Design Process

Aesthetics & Resources

For Terragotchi’s visual language, I aimed to create something simple, playful, and practical. I drew inspiration from education apps like Brilliant, Khan Academy, Quizlet, and Duolingo, as well as the aforementioned wellness app Finch.

The pet Earth is a custom vector graphic sourced from a hand-drawn sketch, animated within Figma, and the Terragotchi typemark is derived from my own handwriting. I used Phosphor for the iconography and Quicksand as typeface.

With UI that shares balanced, rounded lines, and branding that comes hand-made, I aimed to create a visual language that was friendly, playful, and unique.

Iterative Development

I moved from low-fidelity sketches to mid and high-fidelity prototypes, conducting usability testing at each major stage. The testing focused on six key tasks:

  1. Onboarding and orientation
  2. Adding an action
  3. Finding related actions
  4. Completing an adaptive thinking exercise
  5. Logging an action
  6. Viewing personal progress

Testing iterations

Adaptive thinking exercises

The adaptive thinking exercises proved to be the most impactful feature. After reading a potentially anxiety-inducing climate article, participants completed an activity informed by Cognitive Behavior Therapy principles, which helped them process their emotional state and get actionable next steps.

“The article is just telling you everything, and the only thing you can do is absorb… with the app there isn’t a way that you can lose, you can only progress.”

“It’s nice to take a second and ask ’now what?’… it’s a question that never gets asked.”

Action Information

I structured action information to highlight three types of benefits: environmental, personal, and community. Testing revealed that users responded most strongly to personal benefits, followed by environmental impact.

“I’d be most interested to see how does this action benefit me… followed by environmental benefits.”

By showing impacts from individual up to the global level, users could better understand how actions — which they may not have even considered sustainable — could benefit themselves, their communities, and the planet.

“You don’t think about all of the ways that these things interact with each other… I could easily see one of these bullets be something that I hadn’t thought of as a benefit.”

Action Completion

The interaction for logging completed actions evolved significantly through testing:

  1. Version 1: Saved actions remained fixed after logging, but users expected them to disappear like they would in to-do apps
  2. Version 2: Actions collapsed away after logging, but the checkmark icon confused users, who thought it meant the action was already complete
  3. Version 3: Log action button now had a plus icon, the checkmark now serving a role in the completion micro-interaction

Action Discovery

The information architecture involved in finding new and related actions also underwent multiple revisions:

  1. Version 1: Related actions resided within individual action screens
  2. Version 2: Added a “Suggested” tab to the Action Library after I observed users instinctively going there to find actions
  3. Version 3: Flexible filtering and search was implemented after category-based navigation was identified to be creating decision fatigue
